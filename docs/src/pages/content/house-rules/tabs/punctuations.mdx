<Heading>Value based punctuations</Heading>

Punctuations are saving grace! The language as we know is never ending... So, to put emphasis, on certain words & sentences - stick to the brand house rules. See when, where & how to place them.

#### Period

Use it to end the sentence. Period always goes inside the quotation mark.

<Rules>
  <DOs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        Spend more time with patients, and less on
        paperwork.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper> "Hurry up." </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        No more partial views of patients.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DOs>
  <DONTs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Spend more time with patients. And less on
        paperwork.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        "Hurry up”.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        No more partial views of patients
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DONTs>
</Rules>

#### Comma

Use commas to separate items in a series. When listing something use serial commas also known as Oxford comma.

<Rules>
  <DOs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>Lawson, Joy paperwork.</IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        We had coffee, cheese and crackers, and
        candies.{' '}
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        Point-of-care technology for smarter, holistic care.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        Spend more time with patients, and less on
        paperwork.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DOs>
  <DONTs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Lawson Joy,
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        We had coffee, cheese and crackers and candies.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Point-of-care technology, for smarter, holistic
        care.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Spend more time, with patients, and less on
        paperwork.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DONTs>
</Rules>

#### Colon

You can use a colon for three different things:

<ul>
  <li>Introducing a list</li>
  <li>Putting emphasis</li>
  <li>Separate hours from minutes</li>
</ul>

<Rules>
  <DOs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        Make sure your password has: 8 characters 1
        uppercase character (A, K, G etc.) 1 lowercase
        character (h, m, b etc.) 1 numeric character (6, ,9,
        5 etc.) 1 special character (@, #, !, etc.)
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        Remember the old saying: Be careful of what you wish
        for.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>02:00 PM</IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DOs>
  <DONTs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Make sure your password has 8 characters 1 uppercase
        character (A, K, G etc.) 1 lowercase character (h,
        m, b etc.) 1 numeric character (6, ,9, 5 etc.) 1
        special character (@, #, !, etc.)
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Remember the old saying; be careful of what you wish
        for.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>02-36 PM</IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DONTs>
</Rules>

#### Ellipses

Use it to indicate omitted words. But remember not to get swayed away with it unnecessarily. Think, reflect and apply.

<Rules>
  <DOs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>Start typing here...</IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>Please wait...</IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>Loading...</IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DOs>
  <DONTs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Start typing here... in 250 characters...
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Please... Wait...
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Loading........
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DONTs>
</Rules>

#### Exclamation point

A well-timed one can make things feel alive. But try not to overuse it.

<Rules>

      You have successfully uploaded the file.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>

      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        Hi!
        <br />
        Is there anything I can help you with?</IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>

  </DOs>
  <DONTs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Congratulations!!!!

Use it for span or range of numbers.

<b>Keystrokes</b>: option+hyphen = – en dash

      <IconWrapper>
        Australia–USA is a long flight
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DOs>
  <DONTs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        1999 – 2009
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>

#### Em dash

A versatile punctuation that can take place of comma, parentheses or colon depending on the context..

<b>Keystrokes</b>: shift+option+hyphen = — em dash

<Rules>
  <DOs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        Please call my Manager — Jenny Wilson — on Thursday.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        My uncle loves chicken — my aunt loves salmon.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DOs>
  <DONTs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Please call my Manager—Jenny Wilson—on Thursday.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        My uncle loves chicken—my aunt loves salmon.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DONTs>
</Rules>
<br />

#### Question mark

Use it to ask questions, and not unnecessary ones.

<Rules>
  <DOs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        Hi!
        <br />
        Is there anything I can help you with?
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        Are you sure you want to continue?
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DOs>
  <DONTs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Hi!
        <br />
        Is there anything I can help you with???
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Are you sure? you want to continue...
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DONTs>
</Rules>

#### Quotation mark

Use it to show someone else has said it and you can say it too. Single quotation mark is used for quotation within a quotation. Remember 'period' and 'comma' go inside the quotation marks.

<Rules>

      <IconWrapper>
        "I hope you will be here," he said.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        Brian told me, "Diane said, 'This will never work.'
        "
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DOs>
  <DONTs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>

      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>

#### Slash

It has several uses but we use it for writing date of birth to separate lines in prose, shorthand for and, per, or and used for certain abbreviations like c/o care and w/o without etc.

<Rules>
  <DOs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        Joy Lawson's DOB: 02/01/1943
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DOs>
  <DONTs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Joy Lawson's DOB: 02 / 01 / 1943
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DONTs>
</Rules>
<br />

#### Ampersand

Try and use it as less as possible. Use it only when space is limited.

<Rules>
  <DOs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        Mr. & Mrs. Johnson
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        Select region & organization
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DOs>
  <DONTs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Mr. David Johnson & Mrs. Grace Johnson
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Select region, patient & organization first
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DONTs>
</Rules>

#### i.e./e.g./etc./et al.

##### i.e

Use the word 'that is' rather than i.e. (id est), unless you have limited space.

<Rules>
  <DOs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        The fever may last for a short period of time—that
        is, three to five days.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        After work, I’ll walk over to that new sports arena,
        i.e., Thunderdome.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DOs>
  <DONTs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        The fever may last for a short period of time—that
        ie., three to five days.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        After work, I’ll walk over to that new sports arena,
        ie., Thunderdome.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DONTs>
</Rules>
<br />

##### e.g.

Use the word 'example' rather than e.g. (exempli gratia), unless you have limited space.

<Rules>
  <DOs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        Many factors are important, for example class,
        gender, age and ethnicity.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        Many countries, e.g., Australia, Hong Kong, and
        Israel.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DOs>
  <DONTs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Many factors are important, for eg. class, gender,
        age and ethnicity.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Many countries, eg., Australia, Hong Kong, and
        Israel.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DONTs>
</Rules>
<br />

##### etc.

Use the word etc. (et cetera), when listing extensively. Avoid using etc. in reference to people.

<Rules>
  <DOs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        Medical devices (stents, prostheses, catheters,
        etc.) are regulated by the FDA.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DOs>
  <DONTs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        Medical devices (stents, prostheses, catheters, &
        etc.) are regulated by the FDA.
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DONTs>
</Rules>
<br />

##### et al.

Avoid usage of et al. (et alii), unless there are more than two authors.

<Rules>
  <DOs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper>
        "This article was written by Sandra, James, Paul, et
        al."
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DOs>
  <DONTs>
    <InlineMessage>
      <IconWrapper iconType='error'>
        "This article was written by Sandra, James, Paul, &
        et al."
      </IconWrapper>
    </InlineMessage>
  </DONTs>
</Rules>
